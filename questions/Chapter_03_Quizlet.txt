D413 – Review Questions – Chap 3 Answers 
 
1. Describe how data is transferred across a fiber-optic cable. Data is 
transferred across a fiber-optic cable by converting electrical signals into pulses 
of light. These light pulses travel through a transparent glass or plastic fiber (the 
core) by repeatedly reflecting off the inner surface of the fiber (a phenomenon 
called total internal reflection). At the receiving end, a photodetector converts the 
light pulses back into electrical signals. 
2. List advantages of fiber-optic cable over copper-core cable. Advantages 
include: 
o Higher Bandwidth: Can carry much more data at higher speeds. 
o Longer Distances: Signals can travel much further without needing 
regeneration. 
o Immunity to EMI/RFI: Not affected by electromagnetic interference or 
radio frequency interference. 
o Security: More difficult to tap into without detection. 
o Smaller Diameter and Lighter Weight: Easier to install and takes up less 
space. 
o No Spark Hazard: Does not carry electrical current, eliminating fire 
hazards in certain environments. 
3. Light is measured by its _____. Light is measured by its wavelength. 
4. What is the wavelength range of visible light? The wavelength range of visible 
light is approximately 380 nanometers to 750 nanometers. 
5. What are the three common wavelengths associated with fiber-optic cable? 
The three common wavelengths associated with fiber-optic cable are 850 nm, 
1300 nm (or 1310 nm), and 1550 nm. 
6. What is dispersion? Dispersion is the spreading of a light pulse as it travels 
through a fiber-optic cable, causing the pulse to broaden. This broadening can 
lead to signal degradation and limits the bandwidth and distance capabilities, as 
broadened pulses can overlap and become indistinguishable. There are several 
types, including modal dispersion (in multimode fiber) and chromatic and 
polarization mode dispersion (in both types, but more significant in single-mode 
at very high speeds). 
7. Name three physical factors that contribute to extrinsic losses. Three 
physical factors that contribute to extrinsic losses are: 
o Microbends: Small, localized deviations or imperfections in the fiber's 
geometry. 
o Macrobends: Larger bends or curves in the fiber that exceed the 
minimum bend radius. 
o Poor splices or connectors: Improperly made or damaged connections, 
leading to misalignment, air gaps, or surface contamination. 
8. Describe Fresnel reflection loss. Fresnel reflection loss (or Fresnel 
reflection) occurs at the interface between two materials with different refractive 
indices, such as where light passes from the fiber core into an air gap and then 
back into another fiber core. A portion of the light is reflected back towards the 
source rather than being transmitted, causing signal loss. This is a common issue 
at fiber-optic connectors and splices if surfaces are not perfectly aligned or clean. 
9. What are the two classifications of fiber-optic cable based on the diameter 
of the core? The two classifications are multimode fiber (MMF) and single-
mode fiber (SMF). 
10. How does the diameter of the fiber-optic cable core affect the distance light 
can travel? Single-mode fiber (SMF), with its very small core diameter, allows 
light to travel along a single path, minimizing modal dispersion. This enables light 
to travel much longer distances at higher bandwidths compared to multimode 
fiber. Multimode fiber (MMF), with its larger core diameter, allows multiple light 
paths (modes). This causes modal dispersion, where different light paths arrive at 
slightly different times, limiting the distance light can travel before pulses 
spread too much and become indistinguishable. 
11. Fiber-optic cable core diameter is expressed in _____. Fiber-optic cable core 
diameter is expressed in micrometers. 
12. What are the two most common sizes of multimode fiber-optic cable? The 
two most common sizes of multimode fiber-optic cable are 50/125 µm and 
62.5/125 µm. (The first number is the core diameter, the second is the cladding 
diameter). 
13. What is the IEEE 802 standard for Gigabit Ethernet? The IEEE 802 standard 
for Gigabit Ethernet is IEEE 802.3z (for fiber and coaxial cable) and IEEE 
802.3ab (for copper twisted pair). 
14. What is the IEEE 802 standard for 10 Gigabit Ethernet? The IEEE 802 
standard for 10 Gigabit Ethernet is IEEE 802.3ae (for fiber) and IEEE 802.3an 
(for 10GBASE-T over copper twisted pair). 
15. What do the following 10 Gigabit Ethernet acronyms represent: SW, LW, 
and EW? 
o SW: Short Wavelength (typically using 850 nm over multimode fiber) 
o LW: Long Wavelength (typically using 1310 nm over single-mode fiber) 
o EW: Extended Wavelength (typically using 1550 nm over single-mode 
fiber for longer distances) 
16. What is the range of FDDI? The maximum range of FDDI (Fiber Distributed 
Data Interface) is 2 kilometers (km) between stations for the full ring, with a total 
fiber path of up to 100 km. 
17. Why does FDDI use two rings of cable? FDDI uses two rings of cable (a 
primary and a secondary ring) for redundancy and fault tolerance. If the 
primary ring fails or is cut, traffic can automatically switch to the secondary ring, 
ensuring network availability and resilience. 
18. List the five most common types of fiber-optic cable connectors. Five 
common types of fiber-optic cable connectors are: 
o SC (Subscriber Connector or Standard Connector) 
o LC (Lucent Connector or Little Connector) 
o ST (Straight Tip) 
o FC (Ferrule Connector) 
o MPO/MTP (Multi-fiber Push On/Multi-fiber Termination Push-on) 
19. What are some common causes of attenuation associated with fiber-optic 
cable splices? Common causes of attenuation associated with fiber-optic cable 
splices include: 
o Misalignment: Cores not perfectly centered. 
o Air gap: A small space between the two fiber ends. 
o Contamination: Dust, dirt, or oil on the fiber end faces. 
o Improper cleave angle: The fiber ends are not cut perfectly 
perpendicular. 
o Fiber mismatch: Connecting fibers with different core diameters or 
numerical apertures. 
o Excessive pressure or tension: Damage during splicing. 
20. Using heat to join two fiber-optic cores is called a _____ splice. Using heat 
to join two fiber-optic cores is called a fusion splice. 
21. What is required to test a short run of fiber-optic cable? To test a short run of 
fiber-optic cable, a light source and a power meter (LSPM) are typically 
required. This setup measures the optical loss (attenuation) of the cable 
segment. 
22. What device is commonly used to test long runs of fiber-optic cable? A 
Optical Time-Domain Reflectometer (OTDR) is commonly used to test long 
runs of fiber-optic cable. 
23. What principle of fiber-optic cable loss does the OTDR use for 
measurements? The OTDR uses the principle of backscattering (Rayleigh 
scattering) and Fresnel reflection to measure fiber-optic cable loss and identify 
events along the fiber. It sends a pulse of light and measures the light reflected 
back to determine distance and loss characteristics.
