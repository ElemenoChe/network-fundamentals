D413 – Review Questions – Chap 4 Answers 
 
1. A carrier wave is an electromagnetic wave of a set frequency that is used to 
carry data in radio wave-and microwave-based networks. 
2. An SSID (Service Set Identifier) is a unique identifier assigned to a WLAN 
(Wireless Local Area Network). It acts as the name of the wireless network, 
allowing devices to identify and connect to it. 
3. Modulation is the process of superimposing information (data) onto a carrier 
wave by varying one or more of its properties, such as its amplitude, frequency, 
or phase. 
4. A simple radio broadcast consists of a transmitter, which generates a carrier 
wave, and a receiver, which receives a carrier wave. 
5. A channel is a specific frequency or frequency range within a larger band that is 
used for communication. In wireless networking, different channels are used to 
avoid interference between devices or networks. 
6. The main disadvantage to infrared is that it requires line of sight between the 
transmitter and receiver, and its range is limited. It is also susceptible to 
interference from bright light sources. 
7. The two major classifications of antennae are omnidirectional and 
unidirectional (or directional). 
8. B. Omni (Omnidirectional antennas are not typically used in point-to-point links 
because they broadcast in all directions, whereas point-to-point links require 
focused, directional beams.) 
9. The patch antenna (or sometimes flat panel) style was developed with 
aesthetics in mind. 
10. Spread spectrum is a technique used in wireless communication to transmit 
data over a wide range of frequencies, rather than a single frequency. This 
makes the signal more resistant to interference, jamming, and eavesdropping. 
11. The maximum range of Bluetooth 5.0 is approximately 240 meters (800 feet) in 
an open-air environment, although this can vary significantly based on obstacles 
and power output. 
12. Frequency hopping is used to avoid interference, increase security, and 
improve resistance to jamming. By rapidly switching frequencies, it makes it 
difficult for unauthorized parties to intercept or disrupt the communication. 
13. The spread-spectrum technique that transmits data on multiple channels 
sequentially is called frequency hopping spread spectrum (FHSS). 
14. The OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing) transmission 
technique uses the 5-GHz frequency and can achieve data rates as high as 54 
Mbps (as specified by older standards like 802.11a). 
15. The frequency ranges for the three U-NII classifications are:  
o U-NII-1 (Lower Band): 5.150–5.250 GHz 
o U-NII-2 (Middle Band): 5.250–5.350 GHz 
o U-NII-3 (Upper Band): 5.725–5.825 GHz 
16. IEEE 802.11g specifies the 2.4 GHz frequency. 
17. IEEE 802.11n specifies both the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz frequencies. 
18. The acronym MIMO represents Multiple-Input Multiple-Output. 
19. MIMO wireless technology uses multiple antennas at both the transmitter and 
receiver to improve communication performance. By sending and receiving 
multiple data streams simultaneously over the same radio channel, it increases 
data throughput and signal reliability. 
20. The IEEE 802.11ac standard is a wireless networking standard that operates 
exclusively in the 5 GHz band. It introduced significant improvements over 
previous standards, including wider channels (up to 160 MHz), more spatial 
streams (up to 8), and MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO) capabilities, allowing for 
much higher theoretical data rates (up to several Gbps, earning it the "Gigabit 
Wi-Fi" moniker) and better performance in dense environments. 
21. The 802.11 wireless standard specifies the CSMA/CA (Carrier Sense Multiple 
Access with Collision Avoidance) media access method. 
22. The difference between CSMA/CA (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with 
Collision Avoidance) and CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with 
Collision Detection) is how they handle potential collisions.  
o CSMA/CD (used in wired Ethernet): Devices listen to the network, and if 
they detect a collision after they've started transmitting, they stop, wait a 
random amount of time, and then try again. 
o CSMA/CA (used in wireless 802.11): Devices try to avoid collisions 
before they happen. They listen to the channel, and if it's clear, they 
typically send a small "request to send" (RTS) signal and wait for a "clear 
to send" (CTS) from the receiver before transmitting their data. This 
reduces the likelihood of collisions in the first place, which is more difficult 
to detect reliably in a wireless environment. 
23. The maximum achievable distance of a Bluetooth device (typically referring to 
standard Bluetooth) is around 10 meters (33 feet) for Class 2 devices, and up to 
100 meters (330 feet) for Class 1 devices. 
24. The data rate of the Bluetooth 3.0 standard was up to 24 Mbps. It achieved this 
by leveraging Wi-Fi (802.11) radio for high-speed data transfer after an initial 
Bluetooth connection was established. 
25. Cellular technology is based on radio waves connecting to designated areas 
referred to as cells. 
26. Long Term Evolution (LTE) is also known as 4G LTE. 
27. A geosynchronous orbit is an orbit around the Earth of a satellite with an orbital 
period that matches Earth's sidereal rotation period (approximately 23 hours, 56 
minutes, 4 seconds). A geostationary orbit is a specific type of geosynchronous 
orbit that is circular and directly above the Earth's equator, making the satellite 
appear stationary from the ground. 
28. A disadvantage of satellite communication is high latency (delay) due to the 
long distance the signal must travel to and from the satellite. Other 
disadvantages include high cost, susceptibility to weather interference, and 
security challenges. 
29. An advantage of wireless networking is mobility, allowing users to access 
network resources from anywhere within the network's coverage area without 
physical cables. Other advantages include ease of installation (especially for 
temporary networks), flexibility, and support for a growing number of mobile 
devices. 
30. The one major disadvantage of wireless technology is security vulnerability. 
Wireless signals are broadcast over the air, making them more susceptible to 
interception, eavesdropping, and unauthorized access compared to wired 
connections. 
31. The acronym WEP represents Wired Equivalent Privacy. 
32. The acronym TKIP stands for Temporal Key Integrity Protocol.
